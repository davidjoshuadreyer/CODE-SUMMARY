Lab 5: Distillation 
Introduction 
Distillation is a process by which a volatile liquid is heated to evaporation, passed through a condenser 
which returns it to liquid state, and is collected.  If the liquid is part of a mixture, the liquid can be 
separated from the mixture if the boiling point of the liquid is different from the boiling points of the 
other components of the mixture.  One common example of distillation is the purification of water from 
an impure source, such as waste water (rare) or sea water (first described by Aristotle but now common 
in water deficient countries with low energy costs).  Another is the refining of crude oil to produce 
various hydrocarbon fractions such as gasoline, diesel, kerosene, and bunker fuel (used by the Romans 
to produce turpentine from pine oil) or to purify silicon for the electronic industry by fractional 
distillation of HSiCl3. 
Another example is the distillation of alcohol spirits from fermented product, such as whisky (malt), rum 
(sugar or molasses), and vodka (wheat grain).  Brandy and schnapps are produced from wines or fruit 
ferment.  Sometime around the sixth century AD, Arabs, possibly Egyptians, possibly Armenians, made 
use of distillation to improve the quality of alcoholic beverages.  One problem with fermented spirits is 
that methanol (CH3OH, bp 64.7@760 torr, and a toxic alcohol) is produced as a byproduct.  Because 
methanol boils at a lower temperature than ethanol (CH3 CH2OH, bp 78.5@760 torr), discarding the 
initial output and stopping the collection when the temperature increased resulted in better and safer 
product. 
Ethanol boils at 78.5ºC, while water boils at 100ºC.  In fact, ethanol forms an azeotrope with water, at 
95.6% ethanol/4.4% water, bp 78.2@760 torr  If the temperature of the mixture can be held above 
78ºC and below 100ºC, then a separation will occur.  The evaporated material is also a mixture of the 
two components, as both are volatile.  In practice, this is a mixture of compounds depending on the 
respective vapour pressures at the temperature.  A simple distillation will result in a new mixture with a 
higher fraction of the lower boiling material, but not a pure product.  Permitting the evaporated 
material to reach some equilibrium temperature as it passes to the condenser can improve the 
efficiency of the separation, in effect by having a theoretical series of simple distillations occur en route. 
In this exercise, you will compare a simple distillation of a cheap red wine to a fractional distillation of 
the product of the first distillation.  The fractional distillation will start with considerable advantage, as 
the feedstock will have already been concentrated in ethanol by the initial simple distillation. 
Method 
Prepare two evaporating dishes (ceramic).  When material is available, put a small amount of product 
from the simple distillation, and a small amount of product from the fractional distillation on a glass, and 
attempt to ignite the liquid with a burning splint. Keeping 5 ml of the product of the simple distillation 
should suffice for the flammability test. Use the bulk of the product to carry out the fractional 
distillation. 
Set up the apparatus according to the demonstration by the instructor.  Charge a 500 mL round-bottom 
flask with 250 mL of wine provided, add boiling chips (1 or 2).

relative efficiency. Support your claim with data and observations from this lab (flame test, temperature 
profile for both distillation).  If this were an experiment, one would properly conduct each distillation

separation does not occur, as it should with successive simple then fractional distillation.

working with Excel you can copy the data columns into Excel and create your own graph. Use Insert Text 
Annotation to label the two curves.  In the Graph options window, select Manual under Scaling, and 
adjust the Y axis so that only the relevant data show.
