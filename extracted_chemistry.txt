Matter
A gas has no fixed volume or shape. It conforms to   
the volume and shape of its container. A gas can be 
compressed or expanded
A liquid has a distinct volume independent of its 
container but has no specific shape. It assumes 
the shape of the portion of the container that it 
occupies.
A solid has both, a definite volume and definite 
shape. Neither solids nor liquids are compressible.

--- Page 39 ---
Dalton’s Atomic Theory
To account for those laws Dalton proposed:
1. All matter is composed of extremely small 
particles called atoms.
2. All atoms of a given element are alike, but atoms 
of one element differ from the atoms of any other 
element.
3. Compounds are formed when atoms of different 
elements combine in fixed proportion.
4. A chemical reaction involves a rearrangement of 
atoms.  No atoms are created or destroyed or 
broken apart in a chemical reaction.

--- Page 74 ---
If only traces are present we often see 
ppm, ppb or even ppt
• ppm stands for parts per million
Examples:
Rock loaded with 4 ppm in gold means 4 mg of gold are found in a million mg of 
rock (e.g. 4 mg gold per kg of rock)
A solution is 35 ppm in aqueous nickel means …35 mg dissolved nickel e.g. Ni2+
(aq) in one million mg of solution. In such a dilute solution the density of water is 
not greatly effected by the solute and 35 mg per kg is almost equal to 35 mg per 
L.
For gasses we usually use volume! Air that is 10 ppm in CO has 10 mL of CO gas 
dissolved in 1 cubic meter of air. Because pV=nRT we can also use mole, so 10 
mol of CO dissolved in 1,000,000 mol of air

Chemical Bonding
• Interaction between atoms leads to a rearrangement of the electrons to 
a more stable state - bonding.
• The types (extremes) of chemical bonds:
– Ionic bond - electrostatic forces hold together oppositely charged 
ions.
– Covalent bond - two atoms sharing electron pair(s).  Electrons are 
mutually attracted by the nuclei of adjacent atoms.
– Metallic bond -is a complicated type of bond.  The elements are 
envisaged of being metal cations in a “sea” of electrons. The 
electrons are shared between all the ions simultaneously… (Not the 
whole picture!)

• Atomic radius is difficult to define – there is a small

from the nucleus.
• Often atomic radius is defined in terms of internuclear 
distance in a compound.

Methods of representing Size
covalent radii:
• half of the internuclear distance in a homonuclear X-X single bond.
ionic radii:
• The ionic radius, rion, is the size of an ion (due to changes in effective 
nuclear charge; for cations, rion < rcov; whilst for anions, rion > rcov). These 
values are estimated from electron density maps determined from 
crystallographic data. 
van der Waals Radii:
• half of the distance of closest approach of two non-bonded atoms.  The 
forces holding the atoms together are weak London Dispersion 
forces(more to come…).

Shapes of Molecules
• All molecular shapes are based on the 
EGG.
• To determine the EGG:
1. Draw a Lewis Structure
2. Determine # Bond Pairs (X) - multiple 
bonds count as 1 only.
3. Determine # Lone Pairs (E)
4. EGG = X + E
EGG is also known as “Parent Shape”

--- Page 27 ---
Shapes of Molecules
• Once the EGG is known, lone pairs (E) are 
placed in positions to minimize E-E and X-
E repulsions.  E-E repulsions are larger 
than X-E repulsions.
• The Molecular Geometry (aka SHAPE) is 
determined by the position of the bond 
pairs (X).

Kinetic-Molecular Theory
1. Gases consist of large numbers of molecules 
that are in continuous, random motion.
2. The combined volume of all the molecules of 
the gas is negligible relative to the total 
volume in which the gas is contained.
3. Attractive and repulsive forces between gas 
molecules are negligible.

--- Page 47 ---
Kinetic-Molecular Theory
4. Energy can be transferred between molecules 
during collisions, but the average kinetic 
energy of the molecules does not change with 
time, as long as the temperature of the gas 
remains constant.
5. The average kinetic energy of the molecules is 
proportional to the absolute temperature.
